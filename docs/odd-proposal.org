* Executive Summary
This document outlines a strategic transition from the current EpiDoc-centric encoding model to a hierarchical "Family of ODDs" (TEI Customizations). The goal is to resolve the "tag abuse" resulting from forcing Early Modern printed texts and other diverse corpora into a schema designed for epigraphy, thereby restoring structural precision and reducing "spurious" metadata noise.

* The Problem: "Standardization at the Expense of Accuracy"
While the transition to EpiDoc [cite: 1] was intended to create a uniform processing pipeline, it has introduced several technical and editorial debts:
- **Domain Incompatibility:** EpiDoc is optimized for inscriptions and papyri; applying it to printed books (like Holinshed’s Chronicles [cite: 12, 18]) forces the use of elements that do not map to the topography of the printed page.
- **Metadata Noise:** Legacy conversion scripts have introduced spurious attributes—such as =@instant= (intended for manuscript revisions [cite: 1])—onto elements like =<name>= and =<p>= where they hold no semantic value.
- **Tooling Rigidity:** Standardizing on a single, narrow schema limits the ability to use modern TEI modules for dictionaries, linked data[cite: 7], or complex structural divisions.

* The Solution: A Hierarchical ODD Family
We propose a "Chained ODD" architecture that allows for both global consistency and domain-specific rigor.

** Level 1: The Perseus Base ODD (The "Clean Room")
- **Purpose:** Defines the "Perseus Standard" for all files.
- **Constraints:** - Includes the 10 required TEI elements[cite: 13, 14, 15].
  - **Hardened Schema:** Removes global attribute classes like =att.editLike= (containing =@instant=) and =att.personal= (containing =@full=) to ensure a spare, clean base.

** Level 2: Derivative Customizations
- **Perseus Early Modern:** Inherits from Base; adds =namesdates=, =linking=, and =figures= modules[cite: 16, 17, 18]. Tailored for the 140+ files in the Early Modern English corpus.
- **Perseus Classical:** Inherits from Base; optimized for Greek/Latin prose and drama.
- **Perseus Reference:** Inherits from Base; includes the =dictionaries= module.

* Implementation Strategy (The "Harden and Refactor" Plan)
To minimize disruption to existing workflows, the transition will follow a three-phase approach.

** Phase 1: Customization Design (The ODDs)
- Refine the current =perseus_holinshed.odd= [cite: 1] into the "Early Modern" branch.
- Use =<classSpec mode="delete">= to formally invalidate spurious attributes.

** Phase 2: Automated Remediation (XSLT & Python)
- Leverage Python (lxml) [cite: 1] or XSLT Identity Transforms to batch-process the 140+ megabyte-sized files.
- Remove attributes now flagged as invalid by the new ODDs.
- Map EpiDoc "tag abuse" back to standard TEI structures (e.g., restoring proper =<div>= hierarchies).

** Phase 3: Validation and Integration
- Integrate the new schemas into the **Oxygen XML** [cite: 11] project environment.
- Maintain legacy EpiDoc exports as a "downstream" format for older tools until those workflows are fully decommissioned.

* Conclusion
By moving to a tiered ODD family, the Perseus Project will regain the ability to encode texts with the precision their specific genres require while maintaining a "Perseus-wide" level of spare, consistent data. This architecture is open-source, extensible, and future-proof.
